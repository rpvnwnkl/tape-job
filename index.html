BUSY WORK

In this place we are fluff: middle-men, not producers but consumers and distributors. We push the speed that fuels this machine behind us and we judge our success by our ability to sell for more what was made for less and what we didn't make at all. We're middle-men, and we're here in this office surrounded by other offices in a building of offices surrounded by buildings full of offices. We're insulated from anything that might make us think otherwise about these things we're doing every day, day after day, no matter how much the sun is shining or how fast the season is changing or how many people are dying or how little we actually have to show after a day, a week, a year, a lifetime of work.

And there's an anxiety inside us that feels like what we would have felt when those dark grey heavy rain clouds rolled in fast the other day and flipped all the leaves up on trees and then suddenly released and the people in the street ran like wild animals as the streets grew rivers and lakes formed at crosswalks and kids screamed and umbrellas popped open and bent over and cabbies start flying because "man, this is when you make your money" and we just watched from a window up here on the 16th floor waiting for a meeting to start so we could have someone else tell us what our life was going to be like for the next quarter. So no, we didn't feel that. We were busy.

Are you working?

The irony of this experience extends to the day-to-day routines of office life. The physical tools and materials of business, with a different intent, could be used for creative personal expression. The leftovers and errors of this busy-ness could be a balanced composition of textures and materials. And even though we don't see this work it could still be there, remnants of this middle-man industry, forgotten creations from those who don't make but could.
